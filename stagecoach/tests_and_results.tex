\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{LifeCycle.pdf}
\caption{Life cycle used for testing the \texttt{stageCoach} functions. When one arrow exits
a compartment, the transition probability is 1. When multiple arrows exit a compartment, they are 
equally likely and their total probability is 1. Thus, all individuals eventually reach state 7 and then die. 
Only compartment state 6 has positive fecundity, with a 50\% chance of breeding.}  
\label{fig:testCycle}
\end{figure} 

The life cycle diagrammed in Figure \ref{fig:testCycle} is used to do some basic tests of the 
R translations of functions from \texttt{stageCoach}. Calculations are done in the script 
\texttt{testing\_conditional\_time\_funs.R}. 

\begin{itemize} 
\item 
\begin{verbatim} 
mean_lifespan(M7); 
[1] 4.666667 3.666667 3.000000 3.000000 2.000000 2.000000 1.000000
\end{verbatim} 
The function results for stages 3,5,6 and 7 are clearly right. Stage 4 has equal odds of lifespan 2 ($4 \to 7$) and
lifespan 4 ($4 \to 3 \to 6 \to 7$) so lifespan=3 is correct. The mean lifespan for stage 2 is one more
than the average for 3, 4, and 5, $1 + 8/3 = 3\frac{2}{3}$ and the mean lifespan for stage 1 is one more
than that -- both correct. 

\item 
\begin{verbatim} 
var_lifespan(M7); 
[1] 0.5555556 0.5555556 0.0000000 1.0000000 0.0000000 0.0000000 0.0000000
\end{verbatim} 
From stages 3, 5, 6 and 7 the route to the crypt is certain, so the function results are correct.  

Stage 4 has equal odds of lifespans 2 and 4, so the function result (variance=1) is correct. 

Stages 1 and 2 clearly have equal variance. From stage 2, $2 \to 3$ gives lifespan 4, $2 \to 5$ 
gives lifespan 3 --- each of these has probability 1/3. $2 \to 4$ gives equal odds of lifespans 
3 and 5, so these have probability 1/6 each. The function gives the correct value for the 
variance of this probability distribution. 

\item 
\begin{verbatim} 
mean_conditional_times(M7); # rounded to 3 digits  
      [,1]  [,2] [,3] [,4] [,5] [,6] [,7]
[1,] 0.000    NA   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[2,] 1.000 0.000   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[3,] 2.333 1.333    0    1   NA   NA   NA
[4,] 2.000 1.000   NA    0   NA   NA   NA
[5,] 2.000 1.000   NA   NA    0   NA   NA
[6,] 3.333 2.333    1    2   NA    0   NA
[7,] 3.667 2.667    2    2    1    1    0
\end{verbatim} 
Starting from the right: the function results for stages 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7 are clearly right because
there is only one possible route from the stage of origina to each of the reachable stages. 

From stage 2: stage 3 can be reached in 1 time step (probability 1/3) or two time steps (probability 1/6). 
Conditional mean time is therefore $(2/3)(1) + (1/3)(2) = 4/3$, same as the function result. Conditional
times to 4 and 5 are both 1, as there is only one possible route. Conditional mean time to 6 is one more than
conditional mean time to 3, check. Conditional time to reach 7 is equal to mean lifespan minus 1, check. 

From stage 1: should be conditional time from stage 2 plus 1, check. 

\item 
\begin{verbatim} 
var_conditional_times(M7); # rounded to 3 digits 
      [,1]  [,2] [,3] [,4] [,5] [,6] [,7]
[1,] 0.000    NA   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[2,] 0.000 0.000   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[3,] 0.222 0.222    0    0   NA   NA   NA
[4,] 0.000 0.000   NA    0   NA   NA   NA
[5,] 0.000 0.000   NA   NA    0   NA   NA
[6,] 0.222 0.222    0    0   NA    0   NA
[7,] 0.556 0.556    0    1    0    0    0
\end{verbatim} 
Results starting in states 3, 5, 6, and 7 are clearly correct. 

From state 4, conditional time to reach 7 is
one less than lifespan, so the value should be the same as lifespan variance, check. 

Starting from 2, there are two ways to reach 6: $2 \to 3 \to 6$, probability 1/3, and $2 \to 4 \to 3 \to 6$, probability 1/6. 
Conditional on reaching 6, the probabilities are 2/3 and 1/3, so the variance is that of a Bernoulli distribution
with $p=2/3$, $(2/3)\times (1/3) = 0.2222 \cdots$, check. Reaching 7 is inevitable, so the conditional variance
should equal the unconditional variance of lifespan, check. 

Times starting from 1 are one more than times starting from two, so the variances should all be equal, check. 

\item 
\begin{verbatim} 
mean_conditional_lifespan(M7); # rounded to 3 digits 
     [,1]   [,2]  [,3]  [,4] [,5] [,6] [,7]
[1,] 4.667    NA   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[2,] 4.667 3.667   NA   NA   NA   NA   NA
[3,] 5.333 4.333    3    4   NA   NA   NA
[4,] 5.000 4.000   NA    3   NA   NA   NA
[5,] 4.000 3.000   NA   NA    2   NA   NA
[6,] 5.333 4.333    3    4   NA    2   NA
[7,] 4.667 3.667    3    3    2    2    1
\end{verbatim} 
As everyone reaches 7, the bottom row should equal mean lifespan, check. 
As everyone reaches their initial state, the diagonal should equal mean lifespan, check. 
All results starting in states 5,6,and 7 are therefore correct. 

Starting in 4, the mean lifespan conditional on reaching 3 or 6 is 4, check. 

Starting in 3, mean lifespan is 3 come what may, check. 

Starting in 2, we need to check the mean lifespans conditional on reaching 3, 4, 5, and 6. 
There are two routes that go through 3: $2 \to 3 \to \to 7$ (lifespan 4, probability 1/3), 
$2 \to 4 \to 3 \to \to 7$ (lifespan 5, probability 1/6). Conditional probabilities are 2/3 and 1/3,
giving mean $4\frac{1}{3}$, check. 
There are two equally likely routes through 4: $2 \to 4 \to 7$ (lifespan 3) and $2 \to 4 \to 3 \to \to 7$ 
(lifespan 5), so the mean is 4, check. There is one route through 5, lifespan 3, check. Conditioning on 
reaching 6 is the same as conditioning on reaching 3, check. 

Results starting in state 1 should be those for state 2 plus 1, check. 







\end{itemize}
